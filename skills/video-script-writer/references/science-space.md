# Science & Space Domain Reference

## 🔥 Trending Angles
- "The discovery that rewrites everything we thought we knew about [topic]"
- "Why [phenomenon] is weirder than you think"
- "The experiment that broke physics (and what it means)"
- "We just found [planet/particle/organism] — here's why it matters"
- "The Fermi Paradox: every solution is terrifying"
- "How [natural phenomenon] actually works — the real explanation"
- "The scientist who was right about everything and ignored for 50 years"
- "What would actually happen if [thought experiment]"

## ⚡ Hook Formulas
- "There are more stars in the universe than grains of sand on Earth. And we've found one that shouldn't exist."
- "Scientists just detected something from 13 billion light years away. And they have no idea what it is."
- "The animal that cannot die. It exists. Here's how."
- "Everything you learned about [topic] in school was wrong."

## 🎥 B-Roll
- NASA / ESA / ESO footage (most is public domain — note this)
- 3D space animations (planet orbits, galaxy formations)
- Lab footage (microscopes, experiments, equipment)
- Nature macro photography (cells, insects, crystals)
- Archival historical footage (for science history)
- Animated data visualization (scale comparisons, timelines)
- Talking head expert interview

## Tone Notes
- Awe and wonder are the primary emotions — lean into them
- Make scale tangible: "If Earth were a marble, the Sun would be a basketball 26 meters away"
- Cinematic / documentary style fits this domain perfectly
- Use analogies relentlessly — abstract physics needs grounding
- Be honest about uncertainty: "we think", "current evidence suggests", "we don't yet know"
- Avoid dumbing down — trust the audience to rise to complexity
